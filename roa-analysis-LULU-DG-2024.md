## ROA Breakdown (DuPont)

| Metric | Lululemon | Dollar General |
|---|---:|---:|
| Net Profit Margin % | 17.14% | 2.77% |
| × Asset Turnover | 1.39 | 1.30 |
| **= ROA %** | **23.83%** | **3.60%** |

> **ROA Formula:** Net Profit Margin % × Asset Turnover = ROA %
> "How much profit per dollar of sales" × "How many sales per dollar of assets" = "How much profit per dollar of assets"

---

## Interpretation

Lululemon comes out far ahead, with an ROA of nearly **24%** compared to Dollar General's **3.6%** — meaning Lululemon is dramatically better at turning its assets into profit.

The DuPont breakdown reveals exactly *why*: Lululemon keeps **17 cents of profit for every dollar of sales**, while Dollar General keeps less than **3 cents**. That's the price of selling premium athletic wear versus deeply discounted everyday goods.

Interestingly, their **asset turnover is nearly identical** (1.39 vs 1.30) — both companies generate roughly $1.30–$1.40 in sales for every dollar of assets they own. So it's not that Lululemon works its assets harder; it's that Lululemon charges enough to hold onto far more of each sale as profit.

| | Lululemon | Dollar General |
|---|---|---|
| Strategy | Premium pricing, high margins | High volume, razor-thin margins |
| Where they win | Profit margin | Neither — similar turnover |
| ROA driver | Keeping more of each sale | Must rely on volume |

---

## Key Takeaway

Dollar General's model — selling millions of small, cheap items — means it can never compete on margin. Its only path to a strong ROA is turning assets over faster. Right now, it's not doing that either, which explains the low ROA. Lululemon's premium brand lets it charge prices that leave room for real profit, making its ROA nearly **7× higher** despite being a much smaller company by revenue.
